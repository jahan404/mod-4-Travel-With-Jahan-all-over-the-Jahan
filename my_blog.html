Tanguar Haor Tour

Tanguar Haor of Bangladesh, the 1031st Ramsar site, is exceptionally biodiverse. This 10,000-hectare wetland is home to 137 species of fish and 284 species of bird, which comprise 51% and 40% of the total species of the country, respectively. In addition, the 30 mammal, 15 amphibian, and 39 reptile species of Tanguar Haor make up almost 25% of the total Bangladeshi species within their classes.

The Ministry of Environment and Forests of Bangladesh has been managing Tanguar Haor since 2001. Since December 2006, this haor has been managed under a community-based project of the Ministry, implemented by IUCN and its partners, with generous support from the Swiss Agency for Development and Cooperation (SDC).

This decade-long venture has made many positive changes within this ecosystem and the communities depending on it. Three in particular stand out. First, since the 1930s, Tanguar Haor had been leased out to local elites for exclusive exploitation of its fisheries and other resources; now the local people have access to fish under a locally managed permit system supported by the government, which includes a benefit-sharing mechanism. Second, a 3-tier governance structure now exists at village, union (the lowest unit of local government, made up of several villages), and district levels, for the co-management of Tanguar Haor; a part of the permit sales revenue also comes to these community-based organizations for their offices and community development activities. Finally, livelihoods of the haor residents have improved in recent years; participants saved money and used it as revolving fund to diversify their income sources, reducing pressure on the environment.

I also heard about these changes while talking with project participants in the last month of this project. When asked, as the project ends, what their future plan was – three expectations topped their list: in almost all village meetings, they requested for more training on alternative income generation activities, input support, and market linkages for the off-farm produce; they expected more support for their community-based organizations in the villages and unions, including stronger links with government supports; and they very much wanted the fishing permit system to continue to allow them access to Tanguar Haor. Interestingly enough, although parts of degraded swamp forest were restored under this project – vegetation brought under sustainable harvesting, fish harvest moderated to improve diversity and abundance, and migratory birds protected by sanctuaries – improvement of biodiversity conservation did not come up in any of the discussions – not as achievements, nor in future plans.

The reason for not having biodiversity as an explicit talking point lies with the very relationship between the people and the haor; when you were born and brought up by Tanguar Haor, its waves, amphibious trees, fishes, and birds are parts of your life and livelihood − these make you who you are. Declaration of it as a Ramsar site may signify its global importance, handing over it from the Ministry of Land to the Ministry of Environment and Forests may uphold the government's commitment to managing it wisely, SDC's financing and IUCN and its partners’ efforts may catalyze sustainable management through a 10-year programme, but to you all that matters is that your haor has been given back to you. You get its fish, and you live a life that is better than before. You do not separate the wetland's biodiversity as a separate element, but see it as the vein connecting everything.

SUDDEN RAIN

Rain is a natural phenomenon that plays a critical role in the Earth’s ecosystem. It is a form of precipitation, where water droplets fall from the atmosphere to the ground, replenishing water sources, nurturing plant life, and maintaining the balance of various ecosystems. This essay explores the science behind rain, its importance, its impact on different regions, and its role in the global water cycle. The process of rain formation begins with the evaporation of water from oceans, lakes, rivers, and other bodies of water. This water vapor rises into the atmosphere, where it cools and condenses into tiny droplets, forming clouds. When these droplets combine and grow large enough, they fall to the ground as rain. The entire process is driven by the sun’s energy, which heats the water and causes evaporation. This continuous cycle of evaporation and condensation is known as the water cycle, which is crucial for sustaining life on Earth.

Rain is essential for maintaining the health of ecosystems. It provides the necessary hydration for plants, which in turn produce oxygen through photosynthesis. This process is vital for all aerobic life forms, including humans. In forests, rain supports the growth of trees and plants, which create habitats for countless species of animals, birds, and insects. In agricultural areas, rain is crucial for crop production. Farmers rely on rain to water their fields and ensure a good harvest. Without adequate rainfall, crops can fail, leading to food shortages and economic hardship. Rainfall patterns vary significantly across different regions of the world. Some areas receive abundant rainfall, while others are prone to droughts. The distribution of rain is influenced by various factors, including geographic location, altitude, and proximity to large bodies of water. For example, tropical rainforests, located near the equator, receive heavy rainfall throughout the year due to the constant high temperatures and humidity. In contrast, deserts receive very little rainfall due to their location in high-pressure zones, where dry air descends and inhibits cloud formation.

The impact of rain on human life is profound. In addition to its role in agriculture, rain affects water supply, transportation, and even the economy. Cities and towns rely on rain to replenish their reservoirs and ensure a steady supply of drinking water. Rain also impacts transportation, as heavy rainfall can cause flooding, leading to road closures and disruptions in public transit. Economically, rain can both benefit and harm industries. For instance, construction projects may be delayed due to rain, while hydropower plants depend on it for electricity generation.

Rain also has a significant impact on the environment. It helps to clean the air by washing away pollutants and dust particles, improving air quality. Rainwater infiltrates the ground, replenishing aquifers and maintaining the flow of rivers and streams. This process is essential for sustaining freshwater ecosystems, which are home to a diverse array of aquatic life. However, excessive rainfall can lead to flooding, which can cause widespread damage to property and infrastructure, disrupt communities, and lead to loss of life.

Cultural and historical perspectives on rain vary widely. Many cultures celebrate rain and associate it with fertility, renewal, and prosperity. Ancient civilizations, such as the Egyptians and the Mayans, performed rituals to invoke rain and ensure bountiful harvests. In literature and art, rain is often used as a symbol of cleansing, change, and emotional expression. It can represent sadness and melancholy, as well as hope and rejuvenation.

Modern technology has allowed humans to better understand and manage rain. Weather forecasting systems use satellites, radar, and computer models to predict rainfall, helping communities prepare for potential storms and floods. Advances in irrigation technology have enabled farmers to optimize water use and reduce dependency on unpredictable rainfall. In some regions, cloud seeding techniques are used to induce rainfall, providing relief during droughts. Despite these advancements, challenges related to rain and water management persist. Climate change is altering rainfall patterns, leading to more extreme weather events, such as intense storms and prolonged droughts. These changes pose significant risks to agriculture, water supply, and infrastructure. Addressing these challenges requires a combination of scientific research, sustainable practices, and international cooperation.
